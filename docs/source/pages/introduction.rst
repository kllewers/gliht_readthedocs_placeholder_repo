============
Introduction
============

What is G-LiHT?
================

Goddard's LiDAR, Hyperspectral & Thermal Imager (G-LiHT) is a portable airborne
imaging system developed and operated by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center
(GSFC). The current instrument integrates two scanning lidars, a VNIR imaging
spectrometer, a SWIR imaging spectrometer, an irradiance spectrometer, a
thermal camera, and dual RGB/NIR cameras onto a single airborne platform,
enabling simultaneous, coincident measurements of the composition, structure,
and function of terrestrial ecosystems using commercial sensors. See
:doc:`instrument` for how this sensor payload has evolved since G-LiHT's
earliest flights.

Because all sensors fly and collect data together, G-LiHT produces fused,
co-registered datasets that combine 3D canopy structure (lidar), spectral
composition (hyperspectral imagery), and surface temperature (thermal imagery)
from the same flight line — at spatial resolutions finer than 1 meter.

Why G-LiHT
==========

Traditional remote sensing missions typically measure structure, composition,
or temperature separately, often from different platforms, dates, or
resolutions, which complicates combining them for ecosystem analysis. G-LiHT
was built to remove that barrier by collecting all three measurement types at
once, providing an analytical framework for developing new algorithms to map:

* Plant species composition and biodiversity
* Plant functional types
* Fire fuel loads
* Canopy structure, biomass, and carbon stocks
* Vegetation growth, function, and stress

Coverage
========

G-LiHT has primarily focused on the broad diversity of forest communities and
ecoregions across North America, with flight campaigns spanning the
Conterminous United States (CONUS), Alaska, Puerto Rico, and Mexico.
